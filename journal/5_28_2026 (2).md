# Journal – Insurance Smells & Kentucky Corpus Work

## Date and focus

Today’s work focused on stress‑testing the “insurance smells” taxonomy against real-world events, and then proving that several of the most costly policy-layer “phish” are mechanically detectable using public Kentucky materials and live filings.[file:1][file:2]

---

## Morning – grounding the smells in real events

The day started by loading the two smell catalogs: one for **Claims Smells** (41 items across coverage determination, valuation, notice, workflow, subrogation, and bad‑faith exposure) and one for **Policy Smells** (42 items across wording, rating/underwriting rules, regulatory mapping, and spec‑to‑config traceability).[file:1][file:2]  

Research then focused on finding real cases from roughly the last decade where each *type* of smell had already cost insurers money (claims, settlements, regulatory fines, or large legal spend).[web:27][web:35]

A few anchor examples were mapped explicitly:

- **ACV / labor depreciation & magic valuation terms** – class actions alleging underpayment where “actual cash value” and labor depreciation methods were not clearly defined, including Trumbull/Hartford suits and the Cincinnati Insurance settlement.[web:27][web:36][web:39]  
- **Undefined concurrent causation and broad exclusions** – hurricane wind/flood disputes and anti‑concurrent causation cases, plus the UK FCA COVID business interruption test case, where broad “disease” and “governmental action” clauses led to over £1 billion in payouts after the Supreme Court decision.[web:16][web:25][web:85][web:94]  
- **Regulatory/bad‑faith smells** – Florida and Louisiana market‑conduct examinations after recent hurricanes that produced multi‑million‑dollar fines for failures around payment deadlines, disclosures, and denial letters.[web:37][web:59][web:62]  
- **Workflow / SIU bias smells** – Farmers’ “Bring Back a Billion” program in North Dakota (a $750,000 fine tied to incentive‑driven underpayments) and the Huskey v. State Farm algorithm‑bias lawsuit over discriminatory claims handling behaviour.[web:112][web:118][web:128][web:132]

These were then tied back to specific smells like **Overbroad Exclusion Applied**, **Undefined Depreciation Logic**, **InvariantViolation – Payment Deadline**, **Blob Adjuster**, and **Jurisdictional Inheritance in SIU**.[file:1]

## Midday – building the Kentucky legal corpus

Next, the focus shifted from generic smell taxonomy to the question: *what publicly available Kentucky materials are enough to prove detectability of the most expensive policy‑layer phish?*[file:2]  

The corpus components identified were:

- **Kentucky statutes (KRS Chapter 304 – Insurance Code)**, particularly Subtitle 14 (The Insurance Contract) and Subtitle 20 (Casualty), available in current form via Justia and the Kentucky legislature’s site.[web:149][web:140]  
- **Kentucky Administrative Regulations (KAR Title 806)**, especially Chapter 14 (form filing via SERFF) and Chapter 13 (rates and rating organizations), which govern how forms, rates, and rating organizations must be filed and used.[web:145][web:147][web:150]  
- **SERFF Filing Access (SFA) for Kentucky**, which exposes all electronic P&C rate, rule, and form filings since November 1, 2018, including policy jackets, endorsements, and rating manuals, all downloadable at no cost.[web:142][web:148]

This established that, purely from public sources, it is possible to assemble a Kentucky‑specific legal and product corpus rich enough for static analysis of actual in‑force forms and manuals.

---

## Afternoon – picking five top policy “phish” and defining detection

With the corpus defined, attention moved to **five high‑leverage, policy‑layer phish** that have already produced sizable losses in the wild:

1. **Overbroad / non‑deterministic exclusions** (e.g., “governmental action”, virus, data, “arising out of” everything).[web:88][web:129]  
2. **Magic number / magic valuation terms** (undefined “prompt notice”, “reasonable time”, “customary and reasonable charges”, “actual cash value” without a formula).[web:14][web:18]  
3. **Coverage inversion / contradictory conditions** (broad grants silently gutted by endorsements and nested exceptions, especially around BI and special exposures).[web:35][web:88]  
4. **Calculation rule drift / unversioned rate references** (rules that say “current ISO loss costs” or “national appraisal company” with no edition or method pinned, allowing systems to drift).[web:18][web:30]  
5. **Regulatory mapping smells** (generic “as required by state law” or “where permitted by law” with no citations or state‑specific schedules).[web:147][web:149]

For each, a human‑readable detection blueprint was drafted: what patterns to search for in SERFF forms and rate/rule filings, and how to cross‑check against KRS/KAR obligations to show that the smell is objectively detectable with no internal data.[web:142][web:145][web:147][web:149]

Each blueprint was then expressed as **Gherkin scenarios** (Given/When/Then) to make the detection logic testable and executable: scanning forms for certain phrases, checking for missing definitions, detecting unversioned external references, and comparing generic “as required by law” text to actual Kentucky statutory requirements.

## Late afternoon – tying smells to a concrete Kentucky homeowners program

To prove this isn’t hypothetical, a specific homeowners filing was pulled:

- **KENTUCKY NATIONAL INSURANCE COMPANY – Homeowners Policy Program Manual**, KNIC‑127064322, a Kentucky homeowners manual that includes underwriting rules, rating rules, and state‑specific requirements.[page:1]

From this single manual, all five phish were illustrated:

- **Overbroad / non‑deterministic exclusions:**  
  - “Solid fuel burning devices include, but are not limited to … or **any other combustion source deemed to be solid fuel** are unacceptable.”  
  - Dog liability eligibility that bars “dogs that have previously bitten someone **or are overly aggressive**,” with no definition of “overly aggressive”.[page:1]

- **Magic qualitative terms:**  
  - Underwriting criteria built on “personal stability”, “pride of ownership”, and homes “not over‑valued for their neighborhood”, all affecting eligibility but none objectively defined.[page:1]  
  - Restriction rules triggered by “unusual circumstances or exposures” without a definition.[page:1]

- **Coverage inversion patterns:**  
  - A structure where the base HO liability coverage is combined with a mandatory “No Coverage For Home Day Care Business – HO 04 96” endorsement, and separate rules for adding back Home Day Care Coverage and Permitted Incidental Occupancies, showing the “grant → strip → buy‑back” inversion pattern.[page:1]

- **Unversioned external references:**  
  - Protection class depending on “the **Public Protection Classification manual**” with no edition or version.  
  - Insurance‑to‑Value adjustments based on “statistics provided by a **national appraisal company**” with no named vendor or version.  
  - Manual premium revisions applied on dates “**as announced**,” with details in an unspecified “announcement of the revision.”[page:1]

- **Regulatory mapping – positive example:**  
  - Underwriting rules explicitly citing **KRS 304.20‑340(8)** regarding non‑cancellation solely for natural‑cause losses, demonstrating what *good* statute‑linked mapping looks like in a manual.[page:1]

Each of these concrete clauses was then translated into Gherkin scenarios that refer explicitly to the KNIC manual (filing ID and rule numbers), closing the loop from abstract smell → known loss events → public KY corpus → live product text → testable detection logic.[page:1][web:85][web:55][web:116]
